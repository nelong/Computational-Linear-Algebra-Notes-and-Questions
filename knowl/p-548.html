Let \(h_N\) be the approximation of \(h\) using \(\{\phi_0, \phi_1, \ldots , \phi_N\}\text{.}\) For instance, when \(N=4\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} h(x) \approx h_N(x) = \sum_{j=0}^4 a_j \phi_j(x) \end{equation*}
So to figure out each of the \(a_j\) coefficents, let's take the inner product with \(\phi_k\) on both sides of our \(h_N(x)\) linear combination. We get
\begin{equation*} \langle h(x), \phi_k(x)\rangle \approx \langle h_n(x), \phi_k(x)\rangle = \langle \sum_{j=0}^4 a_j \phi_j(x), \phi_k(x)\rangle \end{equation*}
But since each of the \(\phi_k\) functions is orthogonal to all of the other \(\phi_j\) functions (\(k\neq j\)), we will get
\begin{equation*} \langle h(x), \phi_k(x)\rangle \approx \langle h_n(x), \phi_k(x)\rangle = \langle a_k \phi_k(x), \phi_k(x)\rangle \end{equation*}
Thus our coefficents \(a_i\) can be found by
\begin{equation*} a_i=\frac{\langle h(x), \phi_i(x)\rangle}{\langle \phi_i(x), \phi_i(x)\rangle} \end{equation*}
