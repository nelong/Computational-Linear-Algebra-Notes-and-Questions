Example 3.8.3.

As we developed in Project 3, we can define an inner product of
\begin{equation*} \langle f,g \rangle =\int_a^b f(x) g(x) \enspace dx \end{equation*}
for \(f,g \in C^0([a,b])\text{.}\) because integrals are lienar with respect to the functions being integrated.
There is not just one inner product that we could define on \(C^0([a,b])\text{.}\) We could give a weighting of the the points in our region of integration and define a weighted integral which will still give an inner product. Specifically, we could define
\begin{equation*} \langle f,g \rangle_{w(x)} =\int_a^b f(x) g(x) w(x) \enspace dx \end{equation*}
for \(f,g \in C^0([a,b])\text{.}\) For instance, if \(f(x)=x\) and \(g(x)=^2\text{,}\) then \(f,g\in C^0(\mathbb{R})\) but the unweighted inner product
\begin{equation*} \langle f,g\rangle = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) g(x) \enspace dx \end{equation*}
will not be defined for \(f\) and \(g\text{.}\) The weighted inner product with \(w(x) = e^{-x^2}\) exists for \(f\) and \(g\) because
\begin{equation*} \langle f,g\rangle_{w(x)} = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) g(x) e^{-x^2} \enspace dx \end{equation*}
converges to a finite value.
