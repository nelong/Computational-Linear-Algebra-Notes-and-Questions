Let \(g_N\) be the approximation of \(g\) using \(\{\psi_0, \psi_1, \ldots , \psi_N\}\text{.}\) For instance, when \(N=4\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} g(x) \approx g_N(x) = \sum_{j=0}^4 b_j \psi_j(x) \end{equation*}
So to figure out each of the \(b_j\) coefficents, let's take the inner product with \(\psi_k\) on both sides of our \(g_N(x)\) linear combination. We get
\begin{equation*} \langle g(x), \psi_k(x)\rangle \approx \langle g_n(x), \psi_k(x)\rangle = \langle \sum_{j=0}^4 b_j \psi_j(x), \psi_k(x)\rangle \end{equation*}
But since each of the \(\psi_k\) functions is orthogonal to all of the other \(\psi_j\) functions (\(k\neq j\)), we will get
\begin{equation*} \langle g(x), \psi_k(x)\rangle \approx \langle g_n(x), \psi_k(x)\rangle = \langle b_k \psi_k(x), \psi_k(x)\rangle \end{equation*}
Thus our coefficents \(a_i\) can be found by
\begin{equation*} b_i=\frac{\langle g(x), \psi_i(x)\rangle}{\langle \psi_i(x), \psi_i(x)\rangle} \end{equation*}
